\vspace{8pt}
{\small
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{5pt}
\item Firms buy inputs mostly from \emph{other domestic firms}, not just labor --- a cost shock to one sector mechanically hits everyone downstream
\item Imported inputs are the norm (Chile: up to $19.5\%$ in Manufacturing) --- so an FX move is, for the firm, a domestic cost shock
\item Network models get this propagation right but ignore the exchange rate; open-economy models get the exchange rate right but ignore the network
\end{itemize}}

\vspace{8pt}
\begin{center}
{\small\textbf{Does accounting for production networks change the optimal exchange-rate regime?}}
\end{center}

\section{Model}
%==============================================================
%% my slides

%------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{Households}

\small

Representative household chooses $\{C_t,L_t,B_t,B_t^*\}$ to maximize lifetime utility, subject to a budget constraint with a domestic bond $B_t$ and a foreign bond $B_t^*$:

\[
E_0\sum_{t=0}^{\infty}\beta^t
\left[
\frac{C_t^{1-\gamma}}{1-\gamma}
-
\frac{L_t^{1+\varphi}}{1+\varphi}
\right]
\]

subject to 
\vspace{1pt}

\[
P_t^CC_t+B_t+S_tB_t^*=W_tL_t+\Pi_t+(1{+}i_{t-1})B_{t-1}+(1{+}i^*)\big[1-\psi(\hat b^*_{t-1}-\bar b^*)\big]S_tB_{t-1}^*
\]

\vspace{6pt}
Consumption is CES across home/foreign goods, Cobb-Douglas across domestic sectors:
\[
C_t=
\left[
\omega^{1/\eta}(C_t^H)^{\frac{\eta-1}{\eta}}
+
(1-\omega)^{1/\eta}(C_t^F)^{\frac{\eta-1}{\eta}}
\right]^{\frac{\eta}{\eta-1}},
\qquad
C_t^H=\prod_i(C_{it}^H)^{\beta_i^H}
\]

\end{frame}


%------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{Firms: Technology}

\small

Firms in sector $i$ share Cobb-Douglas technology (labor, domestic inputs, imports) and price under monopolistic competition:

\[
Y_{ift}=A_{it}\,L_{ift}^{\alpha_i}\prod_j X_{ijft}^{\Omega^H_{ij}}\,M_{ift}^{\Omega^F_i}
\]

\vspace{1pt}

\[
MC_{it}=\frac{W_t^{\alpha_i}\prod_j P_{jt}^{\Omega^H_{ij}}\,(S_tP_t^{F*})^{\Omega^F_i}}{A_{it}}
\]


\vspace{6pt}
\textbf{Two cost-push channels:} $MC_{it}$ moves with $A_{it}$ (TFP) \emph{and} $S_t$, the exchange rate (via the import cost $S_tP_t^{F*}$).

\vspace{6pt}
The theory holds for any number of sectors $N$; the quantitative results use $N=3$ (Resource, Manufacturing, Services), collapsed from Chile's 12-sector national IO table.

\end{frame}


%------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{Firms: Price Setting}

\footnotesize

Calvo pricing through the network delivers the (vector) Phillips curve:

\vspace{6pt}
\[
\bm\pi_t=\rho(I-\mathcal V)\mathbb E_t\bm\pi_{t+1}+\mathcal B(\gamma+\varphi)\tilde y_t-\mathcal V\bm\chi_t+\Gamma\Delta e_t
\]
\vspace{6pt}

\textbf{Same amplifier, two shocks:} $\mathcal B,\Gamma$ are the \emph{same} rigidity-adjusted operator $\tilde A\equiv\Delta(I-\Omega^H\Delta)^{-1}$, applied to labor share $\bm\alpha$ vs.\ import share $\bm\omega_F$:

\vspace{6pt}
\[
\mathcal B=\frac{\tilde A\bm\alpha}{\kappa},
\qquad
\Gamma=\frac{\tilde A\bm\omega_F}{\kappa}
\]


\vspace{10pt}
{\footnotesize where:}

\vspace{4pt}
{\scriptsize
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{@{}llll@{}}
$\bm\pi_t,\tilde y_t$: infl., gap & $\bm\chi_t$: TFP cost-push & $\Delta e_t$: exch.\ rate chg.\ & $\kappa$: GE scalar \\
$\mathcal B,\Gamma$: PC slope, FX pass-thru\ & $\mathcal V$: TFP cost-push matrix & \multicolumn{2}{l}{$\tilde A$: rigidity-adj.\ Leontief} \\
\end{tabular}}

\end{frame}

%------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{Firms: Production Network}

\small

The \textbf{Leontief inverse} sums direct and indirect linkages:

\[
(I-\Omega^H)^{-1}=I+\Omega^H+(\Omega^H)^2+\cdots
\]

applied to consumption shares $\bm\beta^H$ (Households) it gives:

\[
\bm\lambda_D^\top=(\bm\beta^H)^\top(I-\Omega^H)^{-1},
\qquad
\mathcal M=(I-\Omega^H)^{-1}\bm\omega_F,
\qquad
\mathcal M^X=(I-\Omega^H)^{-1}\bm\omega_X
\]

\vspace{6pt}
{\footnotesize where:}

\vspace{2pt}
{\scriptsize
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}\setlength{\topsep}{0pt}
\item $\lambda_{D,i}$: \textbf{Domar weight} --- sector $i$'s total weight in final demand, network-adjusted (can exceed 1 for downstream sectors)
\item $\mathcal M_i$: \textbf{Import centrality} --- sector $i$'s total (direct $+$ indirect) exposure to import costs
\item $\mathcal M_i^X$: \textbf{Export centrality} --- sector $i$'s total (direct $+$ indirect) exposure to export demand
\item \textbf{Why left vs.\ right multiply:} $\bm\lambda_D$ asks "how much of \emph{final demand} ultimately flows through sector $i$?" --- a question about a row vector of weights pulled \emph{backward} from consumption, hence $(\bm\beta^H)^\top(\cdot)$ on the left. $\mathcal M,\mathcal M^X$ ask "how much of a \emph{cost/demand shock at the border} reaches sector $i$?" --- a question about a column vector pushed \emph{forward} through supply chains, hence $(\cdot)\bm\omega_F$ on the right. Same Leontief inverse, opposite direction of the question
\end{itemize}}

\end{frame}


%------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{Foreign Sector}

\footnotesize

Law of one price and UIP with a debt-elastic NFA stabilizer $\psi(\cdot)$ and an exogenous risk-premium shock $RP_t$:

\vspace{10pt}
\[
P_t^{FH}=S_tP_t^{F*},
\qquad
i_t = i^*+\mathbb E_t\Delta e_{t+1}+\psi(\hat b_t^*)+RP_t
\]
\vspace{10pt}

NFA accumulates the trade balance:

\vspace{10pt}
\[
\hat b_t^*=\frac{1+i_{t-1}}{\Pi_t^C}\hat b_{t-1}^*+\frac{P_t^H\sum_iEX_{it}-P_t^{FH}IM_t}{P_t^C},
\qquad
EX_{it}=\kappa_{EX,i}D_t^*\,PX_t\Big(\frac{P_{it}}{P_t^{FH}}\Big)^{-\theta^*}
\]
\vspace{10pt}

\textbf{Two channels} to the domestic economy:

\vspace{10pt}
\[
\hat s_t+\hat p_t^{F*}\ \rightarrow\ MC_{it}\ \rightarrow\ \pi_{it}
\quad\text{(cost-push)}
\qquad
\hat s_t\ \rightarrow\ EX_{it}\ \rightarrow\ \tilde y_t\ \rightarrow\ \pi_{it}
\quad\text{(demand)}
\]

\vspace{6pt}
{\footnotesize where:}

\vspace{4pt}
\resizebox{\textwidth}{!}{%
{\scriptsize
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{@{}llll@{}}
$P_t^{F*}$: foreign price of imports & $RP_t$: risk-premium shock & $D_t^*$: foreign demand shock & $PX_t$: export price/ToT shock \\
$\psi(\cdot)$: debt-elastic NFA stabilizer & $\hat b_t^*$: NFA (\% GDP) & $\kappa_{EX,i}$: export scale, sector $i$ & $\theta^*$: export demand elasticity \\
$\hat s_t\equiv\log(S_t/\bar S)$: log exch.\ rate & $\Delta e_{t+1}\equiv\mathbb E_t\hat s_{t+1}-\hat s_t$: exp.\ depreciation & \multicolumn{2}{l}{(hats: log-deviation from steady state, throughout)} \\
\end{tabular}}}

\end{frame}



%------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{Monetary Policy Regimes}

\small


\vspace{10pt}

\textbf{Free float}

\[
\hat i_t
=
\phi_\pi\pi_t^{DC}+\phi_y\tilde y_t
\]

The exchange rate adjusts endogenously.

\vspace{10pt}

\textbf{Hard peg}

\[
\hat s_t=0
\]

Adjustment occurs through domestic inflation and output.

\vspace{10pt}

\textbf{Managed float}

\[
\hat i_t
=
\phi_\pi\pi_t^{DC}+\phi_y\tilde y_t+\phi_s\hat s_t
\]

The central bank partially stabilizes the exchange rate.

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Equilibrium System}

\footnotesize
\setlength{\abovedisplayskip}{1pt}
\setlength{\belowdisplayskip}{1pt}
\setlength{\abovedisplayshortskip}{0pt}
\setlength{\belowdisplayshortskip}{1pt}
\setlength{\parskip}{0pt}

\textbf{Phillips curve}:

\[
\bm\pi_t=\rho(I-\mathcal V)\mathbb E_t\bm\pi_{t+1}+\mathcal B(\gamma+\varphi)\tilde y_t-\mathcal V\bm\chi_t+\Gamma\Delta e_t
\]

\textbf{IS curve}:

\[
\tilde y_t=\mathbb E_t\tilde y_{t+1}-\frac{1}{\gamma}\big(i_t-\mathbb E_t\pi_t^C-r_t^{\mathrm{nat}}\big)-\frac{\eta(1-\omega)}{\gamma}\mathbb E_t\Delta\big(\hat s_t+\hat p_t^{F*}-\hat p_t^H\big)
\]

\textbf{Foreign sector}:

\[
i_t=i^*+\mathbb E_t\Delta e_{t+1}+\psi(\hat b_t^*)+RP_t
\]

\textbf{Policy rule}:

\[
\hat i_t=
\begin{cases}
\phi_\pi\pi_t^{DC}+\phi_y\tilde y_t & \text{Float}\\[2pt]
\text{residual (}\hat s_t=0\text{)} & \text{Peg}\\[2pt]
\phi_\pi\pi_t^{DC}+\phi_y\tilde y_t+\phi_s\hat s_t & \text{Managed}
\end{cases}
\]

% {\scriptsize where: $r_t^{\mathrm{nat}}$ natural real rate, $\hat p_t^H$ log home price idx., $\pi_t^{DC}$ DC index (target); $\mathcal B,\Gamma,\mathcal V,\psi(\cdot),RP_t,\hat p_t^{F*}(PF_t)$ as before.}

% \textbf{Welfare loss}: $\mathcal{W}=\tfrac12\big[(\gamma+\varphi)\mathrm{Var}(\tilde y_t)+\sum_i\kappa_i\mathrm{Var}(\pi_{it})\big]$, $\kappa_i=\lambda_{D,i}\varepsilon\frac{1-\hat\delta_i}{\hat\delta_i}$

\vspace{6pt}
{\scriptsize \textbf{Not over-identified:} law of one price ($P_t^{FH}=S_tP_t^{F*}$, Foreign Sector slide) is a \emph{definition} substituted into $\bm\chi_t$/the IS curve's real-exchange-rate term, not an extra equilibrium condition --- the system is $N$ Phillips curves $+$ IS $+$ UIP $+$ policy rule solving for $N$ sectoral $\pi_{it}$, $\tilde y_t$, $i_t$, $\hat s_t$: equal counts, one equation per unknown.}

\end{frame}

%==============================================================
\begin{frame}{Counting the Cross-Sector Term (Rubbo Prop.\ 3)}
%==============================================================

{\footnotesize Christian: \emph{"I really need to count for that."} Rubbo's welfare formula has a \textbf{between-sector} piece, $\Phi_C+\sum_s\lambda_s\Phi_s$ (relative-price misallocation across sectors), that every number so far omits. Now built and computed, not just flagged.}

\vspace{4pt}
{\scriptsize \textbf{Closed form:} both cross-sector aggregators here are \textbf{Cobb-Douglas} (household consumption across domestic sectors $\bm\beta^H$; firm input bundles $\Omega^H,\Omega^F$) --- Cobb-Douglas has Allen-Uzawa elasticity of substitution \emph{exactly} 1 by construction, so $\Phi_C,\Phi_s$ collapse to a \textbf{weighted variance of the markup gap} $\mu_{it}=\log(P_{it}/MC_{it})-\log\frac{\varepsilon}{\varepsilon-1}$ across sectors (only the outer $C^H/C^F$ nest is genuine CES, $\eta{=}1.5$, used for the one domestic-vs-import pair). $\mu_{it}$ added as a pure reporting definition to the \texttt{.mod} files --- changes nothing else.}

\vspace{5pt}

\begin{frame}{How Much Does Peg's Loss Depend on the Risk-Premium Shock?}
%==============================================================

{\footnotesize Adam: "effects of the volatility of the exchange-rate processes." Risk-premium/UIP drives 76\% of Peg's loss (previous slide) --- calibrated at the \textbf{same} $1\%$ s.d.\ as every other shock, no independent target. \textbf{Important, uncomfortable finding, stated plainly:}}

\vspace{4pt}
\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.62\textwidth]{figs/risk_premium_volatility.pdf}
\end{center}

\vspace{2pt}
{\scriptsize
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{2pt}\setlength{\topsep}{0pt}
\item \textbf{With the risk-premium shock switched off entirely}, Peg is \emph{not} worst --- Peg $0.95\times$ Float (essentially tied, Peg fractionally \emph{better}). \textbf{The entire "Peg is dominated" headline rests on this one shock existing}, not on terms-of-trade, import pass-through, or any network channel
\item Turn it on at \textbf{half} the calibrated size and Peg is already $1.7\times$ Float; at the \textbf{calibrated} size, $3.6\times$; at $3\times$ calibrated, $14.5\times$ --- Managed stays cheapest throughout, and its gap over Float barely moves (it absorbs the shock by construction)
\item \textbf{Why $1\%$ s.d.\ is a defensible baseline, not cherry-picked}: it's the \emph{same} size assigned to every other shock (TFP, import price, ...) --- a symmetric-treatment default, not tuned to produce this result --- and sovereign risk-premium volatility of this order is well documented empirically for pegged EM regimes (Broda 2004; Edwards \& Levy-Yeyati 2005; C\'espedes, Chang \& Velasco 2004, appendix lit.\ table). But the ranking's \emph{margin} is highly sensitive to this one number, and a paper draft needs an independently estimated $\sigma_{RP}$, not an assumed-equal-to-everything-else one
\end{itemize}}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Persistence, Not Just Size: Why UIP Losses Are So Large}
%==============================================================

{\footnotesize Christian: \emph{"persistence of inflation leading to higher cost."} The risk-premium/UIP shock's persistence $\rho_{RP}$ (baseline $0.80$) --- crossed with network density --- turns out to matter \textbf{far more convexly} than its size (previous risk-premium slide) or the network alone.}

\vspace{4pt}
\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{figs/rp_persistence_netdens.pdf}
\end{center}

\vspace{2pt}
{\scriptsize
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{2pt}\setlength{\topsep}{0pt}
\item AR(1) variance scales as $\sigma^2/(1-\rho_{RP}^2)$ --- \textbf{mechanically convex}, not linear, in persistence. Moving $\rho_{RP}$ from $0{\to}0.40{\to}0.80$ raises Peg's loss only modestly ($18\to22\to70\times10^{-4}$ at $\rho{=}0$), but $0.80\to0.95$ \textbf{explodes it} to $343\times10^{-4}$ --- a 5x jump from a 0.15 change in persistence, not size
\item \textbf{This is quantitatively the same story as Peg's Services-sector price persistence} (Corr$(\pi_3,\pi_{3,t-1}){\approx}0.82$, "Why Services?" slide) --- a rigid sector facing a persistent shock doesn't get one bad quarter, it gets a slow-moving elevated inflation/output-gap path that the welfare's $\mathrm{Var}(\cdot)$ metric prices in full
\item \textbf{Caution on $\rho_{RP}{=}0.95$}: this is very close to $\rho_{RP}\to1$, and combined with the model's already-near-unit-root NFA dynamics ($\mathrm{Corr}(BSTAR_t,BSTAR_{t-1}){\approx}0.99$ throughout), the order-1 solution is entering genuinely fragile knife-edge territory --- at this one point only, Float's loss is \emph{lower} with the network on than off, opposite of every other result in this deck, plausibly a symptom of that fragility rather than new economics. Treat $\rho_{RP}\le0.80$ as the reliable range; $0.95$ is a stress test, not a calibration target
\end{itemize}}

\end{frame}

\item \textbf{Takeaway}: it's not \emph{how much} you buy domestically that predicts inherited exposure, it's \emph{who you buy from} --- a composition effect, consistent with Christian's original framing ("Manuf.'s import translates into Services' import"). \section{Conclusion}
%==============================================================
\begin{frame}[plain]{Conclusion}
%==============================================================

{\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{6pt}
\item \textbf{The network matters for severity and incidence, not the ranking:} switching it on raises welfare losses in every regime and reallocates who bears them, but \textbf{Managed $<$ Float $<$ Peg either way}
% \item \textbf{Peg is dominated by a different mechanism than the textbook story:} not terms-of-trade, but risk-premium/UIP --- zero monetary autonomy forces the shock straight into the output gap
\item \textbf{Managed float wins} because it dampens the FX pass-through channel without fully reimposing peg's loss of autonomy
\item \textbf{Robust} across three real-data calibrations (Chile, Korea, Czechia) and a genuine second-order solution
\end{itemize}}

\end{frame}
