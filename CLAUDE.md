## Core theoretical contribution
In Rubbo, the DC index is unique because there is one cost-push channel (productivity). In the SOE, there are two channels (productivity + exchange rate). The central result is whether a modified DC index exists under flexible ER, or whether the inflation-output tradeoff is irreducible.

Derives an open-economy DC index (three channels: CPI, expenditure-switching, profit) extending Rubbo (2023) to cross-border + IO linkages; calibrated to WIOD (43 countries × 56 sectors). Key differences from this project, all confirmed from the full text:

  - **No UIP, no NFA, no debt-elastic risk premium** — the exchange rate is pinned by a static trade-balance condition only. Their own conclusion lists **"relax the assumption of financial autarky... study the interplay between incompleteness of the financial market and production networks"** as their *first, top-priority* future extension — i.e. exactly the piece this project already builds. This is the single clearest evidence the FX-regime/UIP/NFA angle is still open territory.
  - **No FX-regime choice** — money supply targets an inflation index; there is no peg vs. float vs. managed comparison anywhere in the paper.
  - **They independently reach the same qualitative DC-breakdown result**: their Section 5 states outright that "the divine coincidence... fails to hold in our multi-sector open economies," via a differently-structured model (Galí-Monacelli trade block + expenditure-switching/profit channels vs. this project's import-cost-share Γ vector). That's corroboration from an independent derivation, not competition.
  - Their WIOD 43×56 calibration is the disaggregation benchmark this project's own "next steps" item (OECD TiVA multi-sector calibration) should aim to match or explain a deliberate departure from.

## Future extensions (not urgent, do not build unless asked)
- **Sector-specific export channel.** Currently $EX_t$ (aggregate exports) is allocated across sectors via $\beta_i^H$ (household consumption shares, Services-heavy: 0.027/0.229/0.744) — the same weights used for domestic consumption — not via each sector's actual export intensity (Resource-heavy in the data: export shares 0.602/0.180/0.036, already computed in `data_calibration/build_chile_calibration.py` as `export_share` but currently unused anywhere in the `.mod` files). So an aggregate export/ToT shock currently hits all three sectors proportionally to $\beta_i^H$, not concentrated in the true exporting sector, and there is no export-side analog to import centrality $\mathcal M_i=[(I-\Omega^H)^{-1}\Omega^F\mathbf 1]_i$.
  - **Fix (if ever pursued):** replace $\beta_i^H$ with a real export-intensity vector $\bm\omega_X$ (the existing `export_share` data) in the `Y1/Y2/Y3` equations of every `.mod` file, and re-derive the steady state in `soe_ss_solve_dense.m` (which currently hard-codes the $\beta_i^H$ allocation for $EX$ too). Once $\bm\omega_X$ exists, an export-side Domar weight $\bm\lambda_X^\top=\bm\omega_X^\top(I-\Omega^H)^{-1}$ falls out for free, mirroring $\mathcal M_i$ on the demand side instead of the cost side — analytically trivial, reuses the existing Leontief-inverse machinery.
  - **Why not urgent:** the export-price/ToT shock is already a minor contributor to welfare loss (0.11/0.18/2.16 ×10⁻⁴ for Float/Managed/Peg, per the Shock Decomposition slide) against totals of 25.47/10.17/102.05 — the headline mechanism (Peg dominated by risk-premium/UIP, sector heterogeneity via $\Gamma_i$ vs. $\kappa_i$) runs entirely through the import/cost side, not exports. Fixing this would add texture to a quantitatively marginal channel, not change the ranking. Re-running it means re-deriving the steady state and re-simulating all three regimes across all three calibrations (Chile/Korea/Czechia) — real work for a small expected payoff. Current stance: note as a known simplification / natural extension if a referee asks, don't rebuild for the presentation.
- **Cross-sector markup-dispersion welfare term (Rubbo's Proposition 3, terms 3–4: $\Phi_C+\sum_s\lambda_s\Phi_s$).** The welfare loss currently computed (`code/analysis.py::compute_welfare`, and the new "Welfare Loss Function" slide) only has two terms — $(\gamma+\varphi)\mathrm{Var}(\tilde y_t)$ and $\sum_i\kappa_i\mathrm{Var}(\pi_{it})$ — matching Rubbo's own `welf.m` `wW` (within-sector) piece but omitting her `wC` (cross-sector) piece entirely. Not a claimed zero: it's untracked because (i) `Φ_C`/`Φ_s` require Allen–Uzawa elasticities of substitution *between every sector pair* ($\sigma^C_{kh}$, $\theta^s_{kh}$), which this SOE calibration never estimates (only a single scalar $\varepsilon$ within each Calvo sector, plus `THETA_S` for the *export* demand elasticity — neither is a cross-sector substitution matrix), and (ii) no sector-level markup-gap variable $\mu_{it}$ is tracked in any `.mod` file (confirmed: no `mu1/mu2/mu3`), so there's nothing to feed into Rubbo's `D2` cross-sector matrix even if the elasticities existed.
  - **Fix (if ever pursued):** add markup-gap variables $\mu_{it}=$ (log actual markup $-$ log flex-price markup) to each sector's block in the `.mod` files, calibrate/assume a substitution-elasticity matrix (simplest: assume CES nests, which pins down $\sigma^C_{kh}$/$\theta^s_{kh}$ from the existing $\Omega^H$/$\beta^H$ shares rather than needing new data), and extend `compute_welfare` with the `D2`-weighted quadratic term mirroring `welf.m`.
  - **Why not urgent:** the within-sector dispersion + output-gap terms are already what drives every headline number and the Peg-dominated ranking; the cross-sector term is a known theoretical gap flagged directly on the "Welfare Loss Function" slide, not hidden. Building it is a real modeling task (new state variables + a substitution-elasticity assumption), not a quick add.
- **$\psi$-sensitivity sweep for the risk-premium/UIP dominance result (2026-07-21, requested, not yet built).** The headline "Peg's loss is 75% risk-premium/UIP" (Shock Decomposition slide) rests on the debt-elastic risk-premium closing device (Schmitt-Grohé–Uribe 2003), calibrated at $\psi=0.020$ (`PSI` in `open_economy_network_chile.mod:158`). That literature has a well-known pitfall: calibrating $\psi$ too close to zero can create a near-unit-root/knife-edge numerical blowup in NFA dynamics that's a linearization artifact, not real economics — exactly the concern flagged on the "Is the Ranking Robust to a Genuine 2nd-Order Solution?" appendix slide (which already checked order-2 vs. order-1 and found ranking robust, loss moves ≤2%, addressing the *nonlinearity* version of this worry but not the *calibration-of-$\psi$-itself* version).
  - **Fix (if ever pursued):** build `code/sweep_psi.m` mirroring `code/sweep_import_intensity.m`/`sweep_import_heterogeneity.m` — scale `PSI` over a grid (e.g. 0.25×–4× baseline), re-solve and re-simulate all three regimes (float/peg/managed), recompute welfare (`code/analysis.py::compute_welfare`), and check whether the risk-premium shock's ~75% share of Peg's loss is a robust feature across the grid or fragile/concentrated near the calibrated $\psi=0.020$ value specifically.
  - **Why not urgent yet:** the qualitative mechanism (peg removes the FX buffer, forcing a persistent external borrowing-cost shock straight into the domestic economy) is standard Mundell-Fleming/fear-of-floating logic, independently consistent with Friedman (1953), Broda (2004), Edwards–Levy-Yeyati (2005), Céspedes–Chang–Velasco (2004), and Galí–Monacelli (2005) — all finding peg-worst under real/external shocks, per the "How Our Results Compare to the Literature" appendix table. The existing order-2 nonlinear check already rules out the most common failure mode (spurious linearization blowup near the knife-edge). This sweep is the one remaining diagnostic — whether the *specific $\psi=0.020$ calibration* (as opposed to the mechanism itself) is doing unreasonable work — flagged directly by the user (2026-07-21) as a "don't want an obvious/known mistake" concern, worth running before treating the 75% figure as fully load-bearing in a paper draft or referee response.
